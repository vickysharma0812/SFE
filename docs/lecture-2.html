Ecosystem

Definition

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms (plants, animals, and microorganisms) interacting with their physical environment (soil, water, air) in a specific area.

Biotic components

The living organisms in an ecosystem are called biotic components. They can include: Plants, animals, fungi, and microorganisms.

Living organisms interact with each other and their environment in various ways, such as through food chains, symbiotic relationships, and competition for resources.

Abiotic components

The non-living elements in an ecosystem are called abiotic components. They include: Soil, water, air, sunlight, and climate. These components provide the necessary conditions for living organisms to thrive and interact with each other.

Ecosystem

Ecosystems can be small (like a pond) or large (like a forest).

A pond ecosystem is a freshwater ecosystem on which different living organisms depend for their survival and nutritional needs. Ponds, due to their light penetration, are capable of supporting a diverse range of water plants.

Ecosystem

An ecosystem can be natural (like a rainforest) or artificial (like a city park).

Ecosystem

Ecosystems are dynamic and can change over time due to natural processes, disturbances, or human activities. Ecosystems are interconnected, meaning that changes in one ecosystem can affect other ecosystems.

Gray wolves were reintroduced into Yellowstone National Park in 1995, resulting in a trophic cascade through the entire ecosystem. This example offers a well-documented case of how the reintroduction of a single species can trigger a chain reaction of ecological changes, demonstrating the interconnected and dynamic nature of ecosystems.

Ecosystem

Note

Ecosystems are characterized by their structure (the physical components) and function (the processes that occur within them).

Structure

  • Biotic components:
    • Producers: Algae, aquatic plants
    • Consumers: Insects, fish, frogs
    • Decomposers: Bacteria, fungi
  • Abiotic components:
    • Water, sunlight, dissolved oxygen, nutrients, temperature

Functions

  • Photosynthesis by algae and aquatic plants
  • Nutrient cycling, as decomposers break down dead organisms and release nutrients back into the water
  • Energy flow, from sunlight to producers, and through various levels of consumers (e.g., fish eating insects)
  • Oxygen production and consumption, through photosynthesis and respiration

Ecosystem

Tansely’s Ecosystem

The British botanist-ecologist Arthur Tansely (1935) introduced the term “ecosystem”. He emphasis on combining both the organism and their environment to describe the complex interactions between living organisms and their physical environment.

  • Tansely’s idea of the ecosystem was centered on the organism, species or community rather than geographic or landscape entities
  • In his approach every organism defines its own ecosystem. In this way, there are nearly infinite numbers of ecosystems. And it is difficult to study such systems.
  • On the other hand, Rowe(1961), Troll (1968), view ecosystem centered on geographic or landscape unit. Organisms are important components oof landscape ecosystem. In this waay, when we study landscape ecosystem, it is implied that organisms, species, and communities are part of such system.

Ecology

Definition

Ecology is the scientific study of “ecosystems” and their interacting biotic and abiotic components, that is, the interactions between living organisms and their environment, including the relationships among organisms and their physical surroundings.

In ecology, the terms organism, species, population, and community represent hierarchical levels of biological organization, each reflecting a different scale of interaction within the natural world.

Organism

Definition

An organism is an individual living entity that carries out all basic life functions, such as metabolism, reproduction, growth, and response to stimuli.

  • Characteristics:
    • All organisms are made up of one (unicellular) or more cells (multicellular).
    • They can carry out chemical reactions to obtain energy and nutrients from their environment.
    • They can grow and develop over time, following specific patterns and stages.
    • They can reproduce to create new individuals, either sexually or asexually.
    • They can respond to changes in their environment, such as light and temperature.
  • Examples:
    • A single tree, such as a red maple, is an organism.
    • A deer, a bird, or a mushroom is also an organism.
    • Unicelluar : A bacterium

Species

Definition

A species is a group of organisms that are genetically similar and are capable of interbreeding under natural conditions to produce fertile offspring.

Examples of species include:

  • Red maple (Acer rubrum): A common tree species found in North America, known for its vibrant red leaves in the fall.
  • White oak (Quercus alba) 白樫の木 (Shirakashi no ki): A large deciduous tree species native to eastern and central North America, valued for its strong wood and acorns.
  • Red oak (Quercus rubra) 赤い樫の木 (Akai kashi no ki or Akagashi): A fast-growing tree species native to North America, known for its distinctive red leaves in the fall and valuable timber.
  • There are more than 500 species of oak trees across the world source.

Population

Definition

A population is a group of individuals of the same species that live in a specific area and interact with each other. For example, all the white oak trees in a forest make up a population.

Community

Definition

A community is a group of different populations of organisms that live together in a specific area and interact with each other. For example, a forest community may include red maple trees, oak trees, deer, squirrels, and various fungi and microorganisms.

Forest

Definition

A forest is a three-dimensional ecological system mainly dominated by trees, woody vegetation, and herbaceous plants that exist in dynamic interaction with the air-earth matrix of the landscape. It is a complex ecosystem characterized by a layered structure of functional parts.

Forest Ecosystem

Definition

A forest ecosystem is a complex network of living organisms (plants, animals, fungi, and microorganisms) and their physical environment (soil, water, air) that interact with each other in a specific forested area. Forest ecosystems can vary widely in terms of species composition, structure, and function.

Our definition of forest ecosystem emphasis its structurally layered and volumetric nature. According to this definition, we should emphasize the importance of geography and physiography within a regional climatic setting to understand the vegetation type, ecosystem structure and function. The forest ecosystem is spatial in nature and is not just the extension of the biotic community.

Components of Forest-Ecosystem

  • The main components of a forest ecosystem include biotic and abiotic components.

Biotic components: living organisms such as trees, shrubs, animals, fungi, and microorganisms.

Components of Forest-Ecosystem

  • Abiotic components: non-living elements such as soil, water, air, climate, and fire.

Energy flow: the transfer of energy through the ecosystem, primarily from the sun to plants (producers) and then to consumers (herbivores and carnivores).

Emergent Layer

  • Emergent layer: the highest layer, consisting of a few tall trees that rise above the canopy.
  • Exposed to full sunlight, high winds, and temperature fluctuations.
  • Trees have thick trunks, buttressed roots, and small, waxy leaves to reduce water loss.
  • These trees play a role in modulating the forest’s microclimate and act as habitats for species that require direct sunlight or high nesting sites.

Canopy Layer

  • The uppermost continuous layer formed by the crowns of the majority of mature trees.
  • This is the primary layer of the forest where the majority of photosynthesis occurs.
  • It has dense, interlocking tree crowns.
  • Regulates light penetration to lower layers.
  • Serves as a shield from heavy rainfall and direct sunlight.

Understory Layer

  • Beneath the canopy and above the forest floor.
  • Composed of smaller trees, shrubs, and young saplings.
  • Plants here often have large leaves to capture scarce light.
  • Insects, snakes, small mammals, amphibians, and shade-tolerant plants.
  • Serves as a nursery for juvenile trees and provides habitat for a range of fauna adapted to low-light conditions.

Forest Floor Layer

  • Location: The bottom layer of vegetation, lying directly above the soil.
  • Description: Receives less than 2% of sunlight and is dominated by decaying organic matter.
  • Characteristics:
    • Dark, humid, and nutrient-rich.
    • Rapid decomposition due to warm and moist conditions.
  • Examples of Life:
    • Fungi, bacteria, decomposers, insects, and ground-dwelling animals like tapirs or wild boars.
  • Ecological Role: Critical for nutrient cycling, decomposition, and seed germination.

Soil Layer

  • Soil is the foundation of the forest ecosystem, providing nutrients and support for plant growth.
  • Location: Beneath the forest floor.
  • Description: Consists of organic, inorganic materials (minerals), water and air.
  • Characteristics:
    • Contains roots of plants, microorganisms, minerals, and decaying matter.
    • Influences water retention, nutrient availability, and root penetration.
  • Ecological Role: Serves as the foundation of plant life, supporting root systems and microbial ecosystems essential for forest health.

Water Components

  • Water is essential for all living organisms including plants, .
  • Location: Water is found in various forms within the forest ecosystem, including rivers, streams, and groundwater
  • Description: May occur at or below the surface depending on topography and hydrology.
  • Characteristics:
    • Supports aquatic and semi-aquatic species.
    • Interacts with soil and vegetation layers through moisture transfer.
  • Ecological Role: Maintains humidity, supports biodiversity, and regulates nutrient transport.

Other components (layers) of a forest ecosystem

  • Climate: the long-term weather patterns in a region, including temperature, precipitation, and humidity. Climate influences the types of plants and animals that can thrive in a forest ecosystem.
  • Microclimates: small-scale variations in climate within the forest, such as shaded areas, moist spots, or sunny clearings. These microclimates can support different plant and animal species.
  • Biodiversity: the variety of species present in a forest ecosystem, including plants, animals, fungi, and microorganisms. High biodiversity contributes to ecosystem stability and resilience.

Keypoints about the forest ecosystem

  • Our definition of forest ecosystem emphasizes its structurally layered and volumetric nature.
  • According to this definition, we should emphasize the importance of geography and physiography within a regional climatic setting to understand the vegetation type, ecosystem structure, and function.
  • The forest ecosystem is spatial in nature; they have both the horizontal and vertical structures.
  • The forests are not just the extension of the biotic community.

Rowe (1989, p.229)

Organisms do not stand on their own: they evolve and exist in the context of ecological systems that confer those properties called life. The panda is part of the mountain bamboo-forest ecosystem and can only be preserved as such. The polar bear is a vital part of the Artic marine ecosystem and will not survive without it. Ducks are creatures born of marshes. Biology without its ecological context is dead.

Major types of forests

Forests are broadly categorized into different types depending on climatic conditions, latitude, and dominant vegetation. The three primary types are

Tropical forests

  • Location: Near the equator, primarily in regions of Central and South America, Central Africa, Southeast Asia, and Oceania. 10°N to 10°S latitude.
  • Climate: Hot and humid throughout the year with high rainfall.
  • They are a subset of the tropical forest biome that occurs roughly within the 28° latitudes (between the Tropic of Cancer, 23°N, and Tropic of Capricorn, 23°S )
  • Tropical rainforests ecosystems are distinguished by their consistent, high temperatures, exceeding 18 °C monthly, and substantial annual rainfall.
  • Vegetation: Dense, multi-layered with broadleaf evergreen trees.
  • Biodiversity: Highest species richness of all forest types, including numerous endemic species.
  • Ecological Importance: Crucial for global carbon sequestration, climate regulation, and genetic resources.
  • Structure: Emergent layer, canopy layer, understory layer, and forest floor layer. Soil is nutrient-poor and acidic due to rapid decomposition and leaching.

Temperate forests

  • Location: Mid-latitudes of North America, Europe, East Asia, and parts of the Southern Hemisphere. These forests cover both hemispheres at latitudes ranging from 25 to 50 degrees. These forests are found between the tropical and boreal regions.
  • Climate: The climate of a temperate forest is highly variable depending on the location of the forest. Moderate temperatures with distinct seasons, including cold winters and warm summers. Rainfall is well-distributed.
  • Vegetation:
    • Deciduous forests: Trees shed leaves in autumn (e.g., oak, maple).
    • Evergreen forests: Include conifers like pine and fir.
  • Biodiversity: Moderate; includes a mix of herbivores, carnivores, and migratory birds.
  • Ecological Importance: Support important watersheds and timber production.

Deciduous temperate forests

  • Deciduous temperate forests: Trees shed leaves in autumn (e.g., oak, maple).
  • They are found in Europe, East Asia, North America, and in some parts of South America.
  • Deciduous forests are composed mainly of broadleaf trees.

Coniferous temperate forests

  • Coniferous forests are composed of needle-leaved evergreen trees, such as pine or fir.
  • Evergreen forests are typically found in regions with moderate climates.
  • They often have an advantage over broadleaf trees in harsher environments. Their leaves are typically hardier and longer lived but require more energy to grow.

Boreal forests

  • Boreal forests, also known as taiga, are found in the northern regions (high latitudes) of North America, Europe, and Asia.
  • Climate: Long, cold winters and short, mild summers; relatively low precipitation.
  • Dominated by coniferous trees such as spruce, pine, and fir.
  • Lower species diversity; adapted to cold environments.
