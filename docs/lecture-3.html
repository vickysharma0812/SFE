Introduction

The development of a single tree progresses from

  • formation of a seed
  • germination of seed
  • transition into a seedling
  • growth into a mature tree
  • eventual production of flowers and seeds

Forest stories come from tree (mainly)

The stories of forests assemble from the stories of the life cycles of individual trees, along with the stories of understory vegetation, animals, and microbes.

Forest

Forest tree variation

  • A forest tree, or any other organism, cannot exist independently of its environment in which it lives.
  • Both genetic and environmental factors influence the growth and form of trees.

Adaptedness

Adaptedness is the ability of organisms to live and reproduce in a given range of environments.

Adaptedness in a forest ecosystem

  • Example: The thick bark of the cork oak (Quercus suber), which is a clear adaptation to frequent forest fires in Mediterranean-type environments.

The cork oak develops an exceptionally thick, spongy bark composed primarily of suberin, a hydrophobic, fire-resistant substance. This thick bark serves as an insulating layer, protecting the tree’s cambium and inner tissues from the intense heat of wildfires. The cork oak’s ability to regenerate its bark after fire damage is a key factor in its survival in fire-prone environments.

Genotype and Phenotype

Genotype

Genotype is the genetic constitution of an organism, which is determined by the alleles inherited from its parents.

  • We can never see a genotype because from the moment of fertilization the genotype is influenced by the plant’s environment; its internal environment of cells and biochemical reactions and its external environment of light, temperature, and moisure.

Phenotype

Phenotype is the observable characteristics of an organism, which are influenced by both genetic and environmental factors.

\[ P = G + E + GE \]

  • P = phenotype, G = genotype, E = environment, GE = genotype-environment interaction

Components of phenotypic variation

  • To what degree a phenotypic trait is influenced by genetic factors (G) and environmental factors (E)
  • For example, consider a dominant tree exhibiting excellent growth in a forest plantation and an adjacent dying tree of the same species.
  • In this case, we know that dominant tree has a superior phenotype, but we do not know if the tree is genetically superior or if it is simply growing in a more favorable environment. Basically,
    • To what degree is its genotype resonsible
    • Too what degree does a favorable microenvironment or the random factor of better handling in the nursery acccount for its superior growth?
  • The important point is the relative degree of genetic and nongenetic influence on a given character.

Plasticity of the Phenotype

A given genotype may assume one set of characters (exhibit one phenotype) in one environment and exhibit a different phenotype in another environment.

Plasticity of the Phenotype

It is the degree to which a character of a given genotype can be modified by environmental factors.

An example of plasticity of adaptive significance is the rooting habit of individual trees such as Norway spruce, white spruce, and balsam fir. Roots develop either shallow or deep, depending on the soil environment, for example, shallow in a poorly-drained swamp versus deeply in a sandy loam upland soil.

Source of variation

  • As we have seen, the variation among phenotypes is attributed partially to genetic differences and partially to environmental differences.
  • The major sources of genetic variation are mutations and recombination.

Mutations

Mutations are changes in the DNA sequence of an organism’s genome, which can lead to new traits or characteristics.

Recombination

Recombination occurs during sexual reproduction when genetic material from two parents is combined to produce offspring with a unique combination of traits.

Source of variation

  • Altough mutations are the ultimate source of genetic variation, recombination is the most important source of genetic variation in sexually reproducing organisms.

Gene flow

The exchange of genes between different populations is called gene flow, and it can also contribute to genetic variation within a population.

  • For example, migrants in the form of pollen and seeds bring to a population new genetic material from other populations.
  • When the population involved are significantly different genetically, the introduction of new genes can have a significant impact on the genetic variation of the population. This is termed as hybridization.

Seeds of tree

Definition

A seed is a plant structure containing an embryo and stored nutrients in a protective coat called a testa.

  • In seed plants (spermatophytes), seed formation is the part of the process of reproduction.
  • Other plants such as ferns, mosses and liverworts, do not have seeds and use water-dependent means to propagate themselves.
  • The size of seeds varies significantly.

Seeds of tree

Seedlings that develop from large seeds (such as walnuts and acorns) have a great advantage over seedlings endowed with very, very tiny seeds (such as cottonwoods and willows).

Big vs Small seeds

  • Successful establishment of tiny‐seed species tends to happen only under ideal conditions of high light, high water, and high nutrient supply.
  • For example, cottonwood seeds are so small that it takes about 5000 to add up to a single gram of mass! Successful germinants occur on recent flood deposits that have the requisite light, water and nutrients. Recent flood deposits dry out as the water table recedes, and first‐year growth and survival depend on whether seedling roots can descend into the soil quickly enough to tap receding water and nutrient supplies.

source: Adair and Binkley (2002)

  • Big seeds contain large supplies of carbohydrates in it which might be beneficial for new germinants. So it might seem that all tree species should produce large seeds.
  • New germinants of pines benefit from seeds that are much larger than cottonwoods seeds (25 seeds per gram), and oak and walnut germinants have far larger seeds than pines (5–10 g for each seed).
  • However, big seeds fall only in the vicinity near the mother tree, which is a difficult place for successful regeneration.
  • Large seeds may be eaten by birds and mammals, with chance of being destroyed (by digestion) or dispersed to favorable sites.
  • Tiny seeds allow for dispersion by wind with the potential that some seeds will land in favorable sites.

Flow of energy in a tree

  • A tree uses photosynthesis to convert the solar energy into chemical energy which is stored inside the tree.
  • The chemical energy is stored inside the tree in the organic compounds, which is used for various physiological processes of plants, animals, and microbes.
  • Through photosynthesis plant convert carbon dioxide and water into carbohydrates, which are then used to build the tree’s structure and fuel its growth.
  • In this way, the biological processes of forest ecology are driven by chemical energy.

Chemical reactions and energy

  • Chemical reactions can be classfied into two categories:
    • Exothermic reactions: release energy (e.g., combustion of wood)
    • Endothermic reactions: absorb energy (e.g., photosynthesis)
  • In Exothermic reactions, the energy of the products is lower than that of the reactants, and the difference in energy is released as heat. Respiration in plants is an exothermic reaction.
  • In Endothermic reactions, the energy of the products is higher than that of the reactants, and the difference in energy is absorbed as heat. Photosynthesis in plants is an endothermic reaction.

Chemical reactions can be viewed as the flow of energy and electrons. In exothermic reactions electron moves to lower energy products while in endothermic reactions electrons move to higher energy compounds.

The electrons cannot be created or destroyed, but they can be transferred from one atom to another. So the flow of electrons can only occur from a source connected to a sink.

Redox reactions

  • The electron flow is called reduction-oxidation (redox) reaction.

A redox reaction, short for reduction–oxidation reaction, is a type of chemical reaction that involves the transfer of electrons between two substances

\[ Zn(s)+Cu^{2+}(aq)\rightarrow Zn^{2+}(aq)+Cu(s) \]

In the above reaction, \(Zn(s) \rightarrow Zn^{+2} + 2e\) is giving up the electron, whereas \(Cu^{+2} + 2e \rightarrow Cu\) is accepting the electrons.

Redox reaction

  • A redox reaction is composed of two processes occuring at the same time:

Oxidation

Oxidation: The process in which a substance is losing the electrons. This process increases the oxidation state of the substance.

\[ Zn \rightarrow Zn^{+2} + 2e \]

Reduction

Reduction: The process in which a substance is gaining the electrons. This process decreases the oxidation state of the substance.

\[ Cu^{+2} + 2e \rightarrow Cu \]

Redox reactions

Reducing agent

The substance that loses electrons is called the reducing agent, because it will promote the reduction of other substance. In \(Zn(s)+Cu^{2+}(aq)\rightarrow Zn^{2+}(aq)+Cu(s)\), Zn is acting as the reducing agent.

Oxidizing agent

The substance that gains electron is called the oxidizing agent, because it will promote the oxidation of other substance. In \(Zn(s)+Cu^{2+}(aq)\rightarrow Zn^{2+}(aq)+Cu(s)\), Cu is acting as the oxidizing agent.

Redox reactions in forests: Respiration

  • Respiration in plants is an exothermic reaction. The chemical energy stored inside the organic molecules such as carbohydrates like glucose (\(C_{6}H_{12}O_{6}\)) is released by breaking down of molecules.
  • The primary form of respiration in plants is aerobic respiration (using oxygen), although anaerobic respiration can occur under low-oxygen conditions.

aerobic respiration

The overall chemical reaction for aerobic respiration in plants is:

\[ C_{6}H_{12}O_{6}+6O_{2}\rightarrow 6CO_{2}+6H_{2}O+Energy (ATP) \]

  • In the above reaction oxygen is taking the electron, hence it is an oxidizing agent. Sugar is oxidizing into \(CO_{2}\) and \(H_{2}O\).
  • Organic molecules store energy in chemical bonds, and the energy is released as electrons flow from organic molecules to oxygen.
  • This reaction forms low energy products water and carbon dioxide.
  • The energy produced is utilized by plants to perform various vital functions, including growth, reproduction, and defense against pathogens.

The aerobic respiration mainly occur in cytoplasm and mitocondria. It contains three major steps.

Stage Location (in cell) Main Process
1. Glycolysis Cytoplasm Glucose split into two molecules of pyruvate; small ATP yield
2. Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle) Mitochondrial matrix Pyruvate broken down; CO₂ released; electron carriers formed
3. Electron Transport Chain (ETC) Inner mitochondrial membrane Electrons transferred; large amount of ATP generated; O₂ consumed

Redox reactions in forests: Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is a biochemical reaction that occurs in the chloroplasts of plant cells. During photosynthesis, plants capture light energy from the sun and convert it into chemical energy stored in glucose and other organic molecules.

\[ ATP + \text{H}_{2}O + \text{CO}_{2} \rightarrow \text{Sugar} + \text{O}_{2} \]

The products of photosynthesis contain only carbon, oxygen and hydrogen. The mass of these three elements accounts for more than 90% of the dry mass of trees and other plants.

Colors of leaves

Pigments

Pigments are organic compounds that absorb light at specific wavelengths and reflect or transmit light at other wavelengths. The color of a pigment is determined by the wavelengths of light it absorbs and reflects.

Why are leaves green?

The sunlight has a spectrum of colors that combine to make white light, and leaves absorb red, yellow, and blue light with green light reflecting from the leaves.

Colors of leaves

Sunlight

  • The sunlight has a spectrum of colors that combine to make white light.
  • Actually, light doesn’t really come in colors.
  • Light (and other electromagnetic radiation) has wavelengths (or the inverse, frequencies) of oscillations.
  • Some chemicals effectively absorb only certain wavelengths, and that’s the case for receptors in our eyes.
  • We have receptors that are activated strongly by light with wavelengths near 440, 540, and 580 nm.
  • The electrical signals sent from our eyes are interpreted as colors in our brains, with millions of variations created by combining the intensities sensed in these bands of wavelengths.
  • The wavelengths themselves possess no property of color. Without eyes, the world has no color.

Colors of leaves

  • The green leaves actually absorb more than 80% of the green light from sunlight, reflecting only 10–15% of the green light.

The apparent color of leaves changes in the autumn as changing chemistry within the leaves reduces the absorption of some wavelengths, leading to higher reflectance. Yellow leaves still absorb green and blue wavelengths, and brown leaves absorb most wavelengths.

Chlorophyll

  • Molecules of chlorophyll do not absorb much of the green light
  • Our eyes interpret leaves as being green because green leaves absorb about 95% of the other visible wavelengths, but a bit less of the green light, so our eyes perceive more photons with a green wavelength than any others.

Carotenoids

  • These pigments are responsible for the yellow, orange, and red colors of many fruits and vegetables.
  • Carotenoids give the color to carrots, pumpkins, corn, and tomatoes.

Anthocyanins

  • Anthocyanins are pigments that can appear red, purple, or blue depending on the pH.
  • They are produced in the leaves of some plants in response to stress, such as high light intensity or low temperatures.

Photosynthesis

  • The growth of forests relates in part to the supply of water, because the uptake of carbon dioxide (CO2) from the atmosphere can be accomplished only by exposing the moist interiors of leaves to the dry air.
  • For each CO2 molecule that diffuses from dry air into the moist interior of a leaf, something like 200–500 molecules of water escape into the atmosphere.
  • When water supplies are low, trees close the pores (stoma = mouth, stomata or stomates for plural) on leaves, minimizing water loss and unfortunately preventing uptake of CO2.

Photosynthesis

  • Plants transpire water at high rates when the supply of water is abundant, and when the atmosphere is not too dry

Rates of transpiration from leaves depends on the density and openness of leaf stomata (pores). Stomatal opening is indexed as stomatal conductance (how many molecules could slip through the stomata present on 1 m2 of total leaf area), and stomatal openings shrink as the air becomes drier. White pine needles and red oak leaves both have stomata that are sensitive to vapor pressure deficits, but the pine needles sustain more open stomata at all humidities (left). The amount of water lost from a leaf depends on stomatal conductance, the water status of the leaf (water potential, Figure 4.3), and the dryness of the air (right). With very dry air, the loss of water can go up even as stomata close down. The pine leaves transpire much more water at any given level of vapor pressure deficit than the oak leaves.

Photosynthesis

  • The story of potentials also explains how water moves against gravity, ascending tens of meters (or even one hundred of meters) upward in trees against the pull of gravity
  • Dry air has a tremendously negative potential compared to the insides of leaves, so water is sucked from leaves into the air.
  • The potential of water inside leaves is lower than in twigs, so the evaporated water is replaced in the leaf with water sucked from the twigs.
  • The twig’s water potential is lower than the stem, and the stem is lower than the root, and the root is lower than the water potential of the soil.

Photosynthesis

  • This chain of potentials moves water from the soil to the atmosphere, by virtue of the very strong ability of water molecules to stick to surfaces and to each other.
  • The potentials in soils and plants are typically negative, which means the potential is lower than in a free‐standing puddle of water on the soil surface (at a potential of 0 MPa).
  • Going from a higher potential to a lower potential means that water will flow down the potential gradient from −0.2 MPa in soils, to −1 or −2 MPa in trees, to −60 MPa (or lower) in dry air.

Growth and Photosynthesis

Plant growth depends on photosynthesis, but not necessarily on the recent photosynthesis.

Growth of plant

Growth refers to the irreversible increase in size, mass, and volume of a tree, involving both cell division (mitosis) and cell enlargement, and ultimately the formation of new tissues (e.g., roots, leaves, stems, xylem, phloem).

Respiration

Respiration in trees refers to a metabolic process in which organic compounds (mainly glucose) are broken down to release energy in the form of adenosine triphosphate (ATP). This process occurs in all living cells of the tree (leaves, stems, roots).

\(C6H12O6+6O2 \rightarrow 6CO2+6H2O+energy (ATP)\)

  • Sugar is the immediate product of photosynthesis, and sugars may be used quickly for respiration needs to maintain leaf cells.

Growth and Photosynthesis

  • Most sugars are converted to starches for short-term storage during the day, and starches are converted back to soluble sugars at night for transport through the plant.
  • In general, the timing of photosynthesis and growth is the same when the climate conditions are favorable.
  • These two processes, however, are not strongly coupled. Because plants have vast storage pools of carbohydrates, that are used over long periods of time to support thye growth of new cells and tissues.
  • The term (NSC) is used to describe these storage pools. They can add up to about 10% of the mass of plants.
  • Most of the C used in leaves and twigs is young, coming from recent photosynthesis. But the C in roots and woody tissues is older, coming from photosynthesis that occurred weeks or months earlier. Some pools of carbohydrates in roots are several years old, or even decades old Hartmann and Trumbore (2016).

Growth and Photosynthesis

Plants use stored carbohydrates and nutrients to grow new leaves. It takes leaves about a week or two of photosynthesis to recover the cost of growing them, and then leaves support the plant.

  • The cost of sustaining leaves can be higher than the gains through photosynthesis if a leaf is too shaded.
  • The capture of light by leaves depends strongly on the amount of leaf area for a tree or forest, along with the orientation of leaves to the sun.

Leaf morphology

Leaf morphology is a result of the interaction between genetic and environmental factors.

  • No single design of a leaf would be optimal for photosynthesis in all situations
  • Therefore, leaf shapes range from cylindrical needles of some conifers to broad, thin leaves on many broadleaved tree species.
  • It is common to find a range of leaf shapes within the crowns of a single trees and canopies of a forest.

Root system

Primary and secondary roots in a cotton plant

Definition

In vascular plants, the roots are the organs of a plant that are modified to provide anchorage for the plant and take in water and nutrients into the plant body, which allows plants to grow taller and faster

Functions of roots system

  • anchoring the plant to the ground (by whole root system)
  • absorbing water and nutrients from the soil (mainly by fine none woody roots smaller than 1 mm in diameter)
  • storing carbohydrates and other nutrients (mainly by larger woody roots)
  • transporting water and nutrients to the rest of the plant

Root system

The root system is characterized by a framework of relatively large, woody, long-lived roots (coarse roots) and a network of small, non-woody, short-lived roots (fine roots).

Large trees depend on large roots

  • The large roots of trees (called coarse roots) typically account for about 20% of the total mass of the trees.
  • When strong winds blow, the crowns of trees act as sails that intercept the force of the wind and transfer it to the stems
  • Trees can only transfer that force down the stem and into the ground.
  • In this way, well‐rooted trees can withstand very high winds, though severe storms can break or topple even strong trees.

Network of fine roots permeate soils

  • On an annual basis, the percentage increase in the size of a tree is usually not very large, ranging from less than 1% in large old trees to perhaps 20% (or even more) in the fast‐growing smaller trees.

  • If the mass of coarse roots needed to support a tree would be a constant 20%, then only small‐to‐moderate increases would be needed each year, with the growth of coarse roots each year accounting for a small part of the total production of a tree.

  • However, about 30% to 50% of the total photosynthates produced by trees flows to the belowground system, not for the formation of new coarse roots, but for the production and maintenance of small (fine) roots and their mycorrhizal symbionts. This will be discussed in next lectures.

  • The mass of fine roots may amount to 5–10% of the mass of a tree, but the fine roots typically live for one or a few years and so the cost of replacement accounts for a substantial proportion of a tree’s production.

Root system

There are two main types of root systems:

  • Taproot system: A single, thick primary root (the taproot) that grows deep into the soil, with smaller secondary roots branching off from it. This type of root system is common in dicotyledonous plants (e.g., carrots, dandelions).
  • Fibrous root system: A network of thin, branching roots that spread out horizontally near the soil surface. This type of root system is common in monocotyledonous plants (e.g., grasses, wheat).
