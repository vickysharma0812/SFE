Stand

A stand is defined as the smallest unit of forest mapping that can be defined as a spatial area where a group of trees is more or less homogeneous in regard to

  • species composition
  • density
  • age-class distribution.

The internal structure of stands contains trees of different species and age

From the view point of forest management the term “forest” denotes a collection of stands that are managed together.

We seek sustained yield of products from the forest, not the stand.

Stand dynamics

  • A forest stand is dynamic and changes over time.
  • Stand dynamics is the study of changes in forest stand structure over time.
    • How stand behaves after disturbances
  • It is important to predict what kind of vegetation will follow regenerative disturbances
  • What pattern of develpment will occur in the vegetation as the stand grows older.

Silviculture is understanding disturbances in forest

  • All silvicultural procedures are, at least to some degree, simulations of natural processes in which stands
    • start \(\rightarrow\) develop \(\rightarrow\) replaced (gradually or suddenly).
  • Plants can only begin growing or speed up their growth when another organism dies, creating space for them to develop.
  • Mature plants often crowd out smaller ones by growing larger, slowing their growth, or eliminating weaker plants.
  • Some plants can endure beneath taller neighbors and thus share growing space with them.

Natural disturbances

  • Fire: fire that can be said to “kill from the bottom up” because they are more likely to kill small plants than large.
    • It is leathal to groundstory plants.
  • Wind and insects: windstorms or pests that kill more large trees than small trees,
    • and thus “kill from the top down”.
    • They have been categorized as “release”.
    • Because these disturbances destroy the upper canopy but allow the release of the understory.

Stages of stand development

  • First stage: Stand initiation
  • Second stage: Stem exclusion
  • Third stage: Understory reinitiation
  • Fourth stage: Old-growth stage

First stage: Stand initiation

  • After a disturbance creates open space, new trees don’t immediately fill it.
  • This leaves room for other plants to grow.
  • Often, short-lived plants like annual herbs temporarily occupy these empty spaces.

Stand initialisation source The practice of silviculture by Ashton and Kelty

Second stage: Stem exclusion

  • In the end, both the space above and below the ground gets filled with plants, mostly trees and shrubs in forests.
  • The trees grow so close together that their branches touch, causing the lower leaves to die from lack of sunlight.
  • During stem exclusion stage, the trees start to compete with each other;
    • the more vigorous ones encroach into the growing space of weaker trees that eventually die
    • The weaker trees die usually from lack of light or soil moisture, in a process called suppression.

Third stage: Understory reinitiation stage

  • In this stage, successful trees start to die due to pests, damage, or being cut down.
  • The gaps created allow new plants to grow underneath.
    • These new plants are often young trees of species that can grow in shade.

Understory reinitiation stage source: The practice of silviculture by Ashton and Kelty

Fourth stage: Old-growth stage

  • This stage begins when most of the old trees have died and younger trees form part of the top canopy.
  • More trees of different ages appear, but a balanced mix of tree ages is unlikely without deliberate cutting.
  • Many dead trees remain standing or fallen on the ground.

Stand development process

The stand develpment process:

  • Starts with the birth of the stand
  • Continue with comptetition among trees
  • Ends with the death of old trees and their replacement.

Rotation period

The rotation period is the time between the establishment of a stand and its final harvest.

  • Teak grown for harvesting: from the sowing stage to the point where the rate of growth of the teak trees has slowed down or stopped such that it is considered economically a better option to fell the trees.
  • Rotation period is the Average age at which a tree is considered mature for felling

Diameter at Breast Height (DBH)

Diameter at Breast Height (DBH) is a standard method of measuring the diameter of a tree trunk at 1.3 meters (4.5 feet) above ground level. It is commonly used in forestry to assess tree size and volume.

Age-Class distribution of stands

  • Foresters need to know the age of trees in a forest, especially when trees started growing at different times.

  • This helps them plan when to cut trees and predict how the forest will grow in the future.

  • The terms cohort and age class are used for dating the ages of stands.

  • Even-age stands

  • Uneven-age stands

  • Multi-age age stands

Even-age stands

  • These are the simplest kind of stand development
  • Trees growing within the stand have only small differences in their ages: It means that all trees almost starts together afer the previous stand is removed.
  • These stands generally contains trees of single species.
  • The difference in age is usually less than 20% of the rotation age.

Even-aged stands

  • Even-though the age is same, the DBH of the trees can be different (due to site quality and competition)
  • Although it might seem that fat trees are always older than thin ones, diameter is not a very good criterion of age and must be used as such with caution.
  • The diameter growth of trees is much more variable than that in height

Even-age stand dynamics

  • They usually appear in nature after deadly events like severe fires or, artificially, from land clearing and replanting programs.
  • The species composition remains pure only when extreme conditions (like soil moisture) prevent multiple species from surviving.
  • During the stem exclusion stage, trees in pure stands compete strongly with each other because their crowns are at the same height.
  • A pure stand typically begins with many small trees (thousands per acre/hectare).

Even-age stand dynamics

  • As trees grow larger, their numbers decrease—quickly at first, then more slowly over decades.
  • By the understory reinitiation stage, only a few hundred or fewer than 100 trees remain per acre (hectare).
  • This continual reduction in numbers happens because trees compete for resources, following a basic rule of forest management.
  • The strongest trees that grow best in their environment usually survive the intense competition for light, water, and nutrients.
  • Growing tall is the most important factor in this competition.

Even-aged stand dynamics

  • The tallest trees are usually the largest in all ways, especially in crown (top) size.
  • As weaker trees get crowded by taller ones, their crowns become smaller and misshapen.
  • Unless something removes the surrounding trees, these weaker ones eventually get completely shaded and die.
  • In this ongoing process, the weaker trees in an age group gradually fall behind, while the strongest push ahead.

Even-aged stand dynamics

  • Very few trees can regain a leading position once they’ve fallen behind in the race upward.
  • This process is called crown differentiation.
  • The relative positions of tree crowns in this process can be sorted into classes.
  • Five crown classes are commonly recognized. This way of classifying tree crowns is called the Kraft Crown Classification, named after the 19th-century German forester who created it.
  • It only works for forests with one tree species, or for forests with different species that grow in height at the same rate.

Kraft Crown Classification

  • Emergent: trees with crowns extending well ABOVE the general level of the canopy
    • So much so that the bottom of the crown is above the canopy.
  • Dominant: trees with crowns extending above the **general level of the crown cover
    • They receive full light from above and partly from the sides
    • These trees are larger than the average trees in the stands

Kraft Crown Classification

  • Codominant: trees with crowns forming the general level of canopy
    • They receive full light from above but comparatively little from the sides;
    • usually with medium‐sized crowns more or less crowded on the sides.

Kraft Crown Classification

  • Intermediate: trees shorter than dominant and codominant class.
    • The crowns extends into the crown cover, formed by codominant and dominant trees
    • They receive little direct light from above but none from the sides
    • usually with small crowns considerably crowded on the sides.

Kraft Crown Classification

  • Overtopped: trees with crowns entirely below the general level of the crown cover,
    • receiving no direct light either from above or from the sides. Synonym: “suppressed.”

Uneven-aged stands

  • All-aged stands are those that contain trees of different ages, often with a wide range of age classes.
  • Multi-aged stands are those that contain trees with two or three distinct age classes.
  • The uneven-aged stands can be balance or unbalanced
  • They can be single-species or multi-species stands.

Single species, three-aged stands (unbalanced)

Multi-aged stand, Source: The practice of silviculture by Ashton and Kelty

  • Uneven‐aged stand, single species, unbalanced: they are revealed as humps on the diameter distribution curve.
  • Natural fires may create such stands
  • They can be pure or mixed species stands

Single species, all-aged stands

Uneven-aged stand, Source: The practice of silviculture by Ashton and Kelty

  • How does such a stand develop naturally?
  • When small gaps form in forests due to cutting or damage, new trees can start growing in these openings.
  • Small pure groups of trees in uneven-aged stands develop similarly to even-aged stands
  • One species that is more resilient to the forest fire can start to dominate the stand.

Single species, all-aged stands

  • These stands typically exist in areas where dry soil or occasional fires limit growth to just one tree species.
  • Many ponderosa pine forests in the western interior show this pattern
  • Most all-aged stands actually contain multiple species.
  • Managing forests with trees of different ages is challenging, especially when aiming for steady wood production.
  • Foresters often adjust tree sizes to create specific patterns.

Single aged, stratified mixtures

  • Stratified mixtures develop differently than simple pure even-aged stands.
  • Trees grow to different heights, forming distinct layers or stories in the forest canopy.
  • Each layer typically contains one species or similar species.

Single aged, stratified mixtures

  • The sorting into strata starts early when the forest begins growing and becomes most obvious when trees crowd together.
  • Trees compete most strongly with similar trees in the same layer.
  • Lower tree layers survive alongside upper ones.
  • Species in lower layers are adapted to live there without trying to reach the top.
  • Species that were tall in previous forests usually grow tall again, even if this happens later when the forest becomes crowded.

Stand structural objectives

  • To maintain or create a forest stand of certain age-class structure
  • Even-aged management is particularly suitable when Timber production is a primary objective or when operational efficiency is prioritized over structural diversity.
    • Uniform growth rates make planning and operations more predictable
    • Standardized silvicultural treatments can be applied across the entire stand
    • Concentrated harvesting reduces operational costs
    • Higher volume yield per area during harvest
    • More consistent wood quality and product dimensions
    • Creates optimal growing conditions for desired species with similar growth requirements

Stand structural objectives

Uneven-aged forest

  • Useful when we are managing forests for the purpose of wildlife or promoting biodiversity.
  • Young saplings provide food for herbivores and old snag trees provide shelter to many birds
  • More robust against pests, diseases, and climate fluctuations
  • Reduced risk of catastrophic disturbances affecting the entire stand
  • Creates a more regular cash flow rather than periodic large payments
  • Continuous forest cover reduces soil erosion
  • Better maintains water quality and regulates water flow
  • Maintains more consistent carbon stocks over time
